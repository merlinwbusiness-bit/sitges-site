Eleven beaches, eleven moods

The coastline runs for kilometers, but each stretch has its own character — find the one that matches yours.

Platja de Sant Sebastià

Family · Old Town

Tucked beside the historic centre, calm water and a lively promenade make this the classic first stop — close enough to walk to from anywhere downtown.

Platja de la Bassa Rodona

LGBTQ+ · Lively

The main gay-friendly beach, busy and social through summer, right in the heart of the action.

Platja del Balmins

Clothing-optional · Cove

Past the marina toward Port d'Aiguadolç, a small cove known for its relaxed, clothing-optional atmosphere.

Platja de Terramar

Calm waters · West end

Rocky dykes create a sheltered lagoon — one of the safest spots for swimming, with a golf club just behind it.

Platja de la Ribera

Family · East side

Near the Sant Bartomeu i Tecla church, a favourite for families staying on the eastern edge of town.

Cala Morisca

Hidden · Quiet escape

Outside the main strip, a small wild cove surrounded by Garraf cliffs — bring water and shoes for the path, leave the crowds behind.

The artists' town

In the late 1800s, painters and architects fell for the light here — and never quite left. Their houses are now museums.

I

Cau Ferrat Museum

The former home and studio of painter Santiago Rusiñol, packed with wrought iron, ceramics, and works by Rusiñol himself — plus a couple of pieces by a young friend named Picasso.

II

Palau de Maricel

A early-20th-century palace mixing Gothic and Renaissance touches in a distinctly Catalan Noucentista style — courtyards, colour, and sea views. Guided visits run weekly, so book ahead.

III

Església de Sant Bartomeu i Santa Tecla

The baroque church on the rocky point above the beach — Sitges' most photographed silhouette, especially at sunset.

IV

The Old Town

White-washed houses, narrow lanes, and small squares built for getting pleasantly lost — every pass turns up something new.

Two faces of one town

Sitges hosts some of the liveliest festivals in Europe — and they couldn't be more different from each other.

Carnival

February · ~10 days before Lent

One of the longest, most flamboyant Carnivals in Spain — costume parades, all-night parties, and a town that fully commits to the bit.

Sitges Film Festival

October

The world's leading festival for fantasy and horror cinema — red carpets, late screenings, and a global cult following descending on a small beach town.

Eat like the coast

Catalan seafood, rice dishes built for sharing, and some of the world's oldest cava cellars just inland.

Arròs caldós

A brothy, almost-soupy rice dish loaded with seafood — the local answer to paella, eaten with a spoon.

Fresh-caught seafood

Small streets near the seafront hide family-run spots specialising in the day's catch — simple grilling, big flavour.

Cava country

The Penedès DO is minutes away — home to historic houses producing cava by traditional méthode, including some of the oldest family wineries in the world.

Rooftop & sea views

Lunch on a rooftop terrace overlooking the Mediterranean is practically a Sitges rite of passage.
